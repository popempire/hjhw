I recently finished *Story of a Soul* by St. Therese of Lisieux.
I found it interesting and inspiring.
St. Therese is unique — on one hand she exudes such innocence and extreme humility,
and on the other she has an intense taste for the macabre and speaks, at times,
with an undeniable native authority (which reminds one of what is said about
the child Jesus, speaking with authority on the scriptures among the scribes and priests).

In one passage — chapter 10, from the third of three manuscripts that comprise the book,
written about three months before her death — Therese describes in delicate language
a test of faith, a dark night of the soul, which she continues to experience at the
time of writing. This darkness has robbed her of all the *feeling* of believing
in heaven. The voice that speaks against her in her own mind mocks her as she 
approaches, not eternal paradise, but oblivion. Therese says she might not have
been able to handle this challenge if had happened to her earlier. But as it
happened, she was so set in her ways of pursuing sainthood that she continued on
the path she had committed to, while losing the joy and *desire* she would have
felt in approaching heaven as her illness progressed. It seems clear to her that
this was yet one more desire that had to be refined out of her by divine grace
in the process of subsuming her life in Jesus — I find it very interesting how
Christian mysticism, on certain points, seems to converge with many other 
mystical or contemplative faiths, including Buddhism.
